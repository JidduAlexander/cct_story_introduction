Current state of warming

Global warming refers to the change in Earth's average surface temperature compared to pre-industrial times (roughly 1850-1900, before large-scale fossil fuel use).

Nations agreed together in 2015 in the Paris agreement that this warming should stay well below 2¬∞C, with efforts to limit it to 1.5¬∞C.

Not only the Earth surface is warming, the complete globe (oceans, land, atmosphere) is warming due to an energy imbalance. You can read more about that in the box.

The Earth is warming up because there is an energy imbalance with space. More energy is entering our globe from space than that the Earth is radiating back out. This extra energy results in a warmer Earth.

There are multiple ways to represent the warming of the Earth surface, the most common is observed warming, the preferred one is called human-induced warming, both are shown in the chart. Currently the most recent value for human induced warming is 1.36¬∞C for the year 2024. The difference between observed and human-induced warming is another complex topic that you can read more about in the following box.

Observed warming are measured from temperature stations, satellites, and ocean buoys worldwide. This observed warming bounces around from year to year. El Ni√±o events temporarily spike temperatures. Volcanic eruptions briefly cool things down. Ocean circulation patterns shift heat between the atmosphere and deep ocean. These natural fluctuations create noise in the signal, making it harder to see the underlying trend driven by human activities.

To cut through this noise, scientists track human-induced warming‚Äîa metric that filters out natural fluctuations to show the underlying warming trend that human activities are causing. Think of it as the signal beneath the noise, the steady upward march that continues regardless of whether a particular year is affected by El Ni√±o or La Ni√±a.

This steady rate allows us not to panic or relax due to yearly peaks or dips. Climate change is long term issue that shouldn't be viewed differently from year to year. The steady pace also gives us easier predictive power to see where we are heading. This makes the human-induced warming a preferred metric in this case, however observed warming is still an important metric highlighting the actual state.

Human-induced warming is increasing at approximately 0.27¬∞C per decade. If we continue on our current trajectory we'll reach 1.5¬∞C of human-induced warming around 2030, and hit 2.0¬∞C by 2048.

Impacts of a warming world

At warmer temperatures the impacts gets significantly worse.

For example, at 1.5¬∞C, coral reefs face 70-90% decline and 280 million more people experience extreme heat annually. At 1.7¬∞C we see near-complete loss of reefs and 420 million exposed to dangerous heat. Cross 2¬∞C and coral reefs functionally go extinct, 700 million face deadly heat conditions, and failures in major farming regions. By 3¬∞C, we're looking at regions becoming uninhabitable, widespread food system collapse, and threats to civilizational stability.

These aren't distant possibilities‚Äîthey're the physical consequences of temperature thresholds we're approaching this century. Use the interactive explorer on the right to see the detailed impacts at each level.

Greenhouse gas emissions

There are many factors influencing the warming of our planet, greenhouse gas emissions like CO‚ÇÇ, methane, nitrous oxide and F-gases, aerosols, ozone, albedo changes with snow, ice and deforestation, and many more. See the box for a more indepth introduction.

...

CO‚ÇÇ emissions from human activity are the largest contributor of global warming. What makes CO‚ÇÇ even more significant: warming has been nearly perfectly linear with cumulative CO‚ÇÇ emissions. This means that scientist can calculate with reasonable precision how much more CO‚ÇÇ we can emit before reaching any temperature threshold. This is what we call remaining carbon budgets.

  • Emitting another 130 Gt from now brings us to 1.5¬∞C
  • 490 Gt emissions and we reach 1.7¬∞C
  • 1050 Gt total for 2.0¬∞C

Carbon budgets come at different likelihoods, the above values are at 50% likelihood. If you'd like to understand this and other details about the carbon budget you can go into the indepth box.

Likelihoods: The Remaining Carbon Budget is not just a single number. Scientists calculate budgets for a range of likelihoods and limits. The limits are different temperatures, like 1.5¬∞C, 1.7¬∞C and 2.0¬∞C. The likelihood goes from 0% to 100%. The likelihood is like an uncertainty range for the scientist's calculation. For example, remaining carbon budget for 50% likelihood for the 1.5¬∞C limit is 130Gt. The scientist isn't sure that after 130Gt the warming will be exactly 1.5¬∞C, he/she gives it a 50/50 chance that the temperature will be above or below. If you want a 67% chance to stay below with only 33% of going over the temperature limit you have to stick to the 67% likelihood budget for 1.5¬∞C which is 80Gt.

Non-CO‚ÇÇ Warming:The remaining carbon budget is specific to CO‚ÇÇ. In calculating these budgets scientists assumes a certain amount of warming from other sources like methane, nitrous oxide, and aerosol pollution. If these sources warm more than expected, our CO‚ÇÇ budget shrinks; if they warm less or cool, we have more room. The carbon budget is essentially a moving target that shifts as these other factors change. Climate change tracker follows a yearly updated analysis and scientist keep very much on top of this topic.

From the CO‚ÇÇ emissions chart you can see that global CO‚ÇÇ emissions are at roughly 40Gt per year. As it shows in the Remaining carbon budget widget that means at this rate the budget is gone in 2028. The actual warming from emissions has a delay, as mentioned much earlier the expected year to reach 1.5¬∞C is 2030.

Emission pathways

To stay below 1.5¬∞C, we would need to go from 40Gt per year to 0Gt per year before reaching a total of 130Gt emissions, that means approximately 17% annual emissions reductions (6Gt), reaching net-zero CO‚ÇÇ by 2031. This would require an immediate, global mobilization unlike anything attempted before‚Äîfaster than wartime industrial transformations. With current emissions flat or rising in many regions, this isn't happening.

But that doesn't mean the 1.5¬∞C threshold is meaningless or that we should abandon it as a goal. It is possible to temporarily exceed 1.5¬∞C (an "overshoot") and return to it later this century by removing CO‚ÇÇ from the atmosphere.

If you want more understanding of net-zero and carbon removal see the box.

The term "net-zero" appears constantly in climate discussions, but it's worth understanding what it actually means and why it matters.

Net-Zero CO‚ÇÇ: This is the critical threshold for stabilizing temperature. When we emit exactly as much CO‚ÇÇ as we remove from the atmosphere (either through natural sinks or technological removal), the atmospheric CO‚ÇÇ concentration stops rising, and temperature stops increasing. This is why net-zero CO‚ÇÇ specifically‚Äînot just overall net-zero greenhouse gases‚Äîis the target for halting warming.

Natural Carbon Sinks: Earth's land and ocean currently absorb about half of our CO‚ÇÇ emissions. Forests, soils, and phytoplankton pull CO‚ÇÇ from the air; oceans dissolve it into seawater. These natural processes are enormous‚Äîwithout them, atmospheric CO‚ÇÇ would be roughly double what it is today. But these sinks are under stress. As oceans warm, they absorb less CO‚ÇÇ. As forests face heat stress and drought, they grow more slowly or die. At higher warming levels, the fraction of emissions that natural sinks can absorb may decline, making net-zero harder to achieve.

Gross vs. Net Emissions: Gross emissions are what we put into the atmosphere (currently around 40 Gt CO‚ÇÇ/year from fossil fuels and land use). Net emissions are what remains after natural sinks absorb their share (currently around 20 Gt CO‚ÇÇ/year). Net-zero means bringing net emissions to zero‚Äîeither by reducing gross emissions to the level that natural sinks can handle, or by actively removing CO‚ÇÇ to compensate for continued gross emissions, or some combination.

Human CO‚ÇÇ Removal: Beyond natural sinks, we can enhance CO‚ÇÇ removal through: reforestation and improved land management (using nature more effectively); direct air capture (machines that chemically extract CO‚ÇÇ from air); enhanced weathering (spreading minerals that react with and sequester CO‚ÇÇ); ocean alkalinity enhancement (increasing the ocean's capacity to absorb CO‚ÇÇ). All of these are at different stages of development, with different costs, scales, and side effects. None are currently deployed at climate-relevant scales, though many are promising.

Net-Zero Greenhouse Gases: This is a broader target that includes methane, nitrous oxide, and other greenhouse gases alongside CO‚ÇÇ. Because these gases have different atmospheric lifetimes and warming effects, "net-zero GHG" is more complex than net-zero CO‚ÇÇ. Typically, it involves deep reductions in all greenhouse gases with some residual emissions balanced by removals.

The key insight: we must reach net-zero CO‚ÇÇ to stop temperatures from rising. The faster we get there, the lower the peak temperature. Every year of delay means more warming to stabilize at a higher level.

For 1.7¬∞C without overshoot, we need approximately 4% annual emissions reduction, reaching net-zero by 2049.

This is technically and economically achievable with technologies we have or are rapidly developing. However, this is still very ambitious when compared to our current situation.

Global and national efforts

In the CO‚ÇÇ emissions chart you can see that global emissions are relatively flat in the last 12 years. They are definitely not dropping with 4% per year. That would have to happen very quickly from now to keep even the 1.7¬∞C limit (well below 2¬∞C) manageable.

Let's dive into national emissions to determine what needs to happen.

High-income nations (North America, Europe, Australia, Japan, South Korea): These nations are responsible for roughly 60% of historical CO‚ÇÇ emissions while representing only about 16% of global population. Most are already seeing emissions decline at 1-3% annually due to renewable energy growth and industrial changes. They have the technological capacity, financial resources, and infrastructure to move faster. We at Climate Change Tracker believe they should either:

  • Accelerate domestic reductions to 6-8% annually, to provide developing nations time to reduce emissions, or
  • maintain 4% domestic reductions while funding developing nations to reduce emissions and develop at the same time

Middle-income emerging economies (China, much of Southeast Asia, Latin America, Eastern Europe): These nations have developed rapidly over recent decades, with emissions growth accounting for most of the global increase since 2000. They face the challenge of providing prosperity to large populations while transitioning their energy systems‚Äîdoing in 20 years what developed nations did over a century. They need to peak emissions soon and work toward 4% reductions as fast as possible.

Lower-income nations (much of Africa, South Asia, small island states): These countries contributed least to historical emissions. They're still building basic infrastructure and lifting populations from poverty‚Äîdevelopment that historically has been energy-intensive. They either need to be given time before reducing emissions or given the funds and support to develop and reduce at the same time.

On top of the costs to reduce emissions there are also the costs to adapt to the impacts of climate change. As is shown above in the temperature impact explorer, these cost rise quickly at higher temperatures. It is the mostly the lower-income nations that bare the highest consequences and costs. These should shared fairly too.
